Executive Summary

The Challenge: Breaking Through Legacy Tool Resistance

RADAR was a critical remote diagnostics platform used by 15,000+ service engineers and technical experts globally to troubleshoot medical imaging systems (MRI, CT, X-Ray). The tool had remained unchanged for 10 years, resulting in inefficient workflows, high operational costs, and significant engineer frustration.

As Lead UX Designer managing a team of 4 designers, I led the complete redesign from research through implementation. The challenge wasn't just improving the interface—it was fundamentally rethinking how engineers diagnosed complex medical equipment failures while overcoming deep-rooted resistance to change from users who had spent a decade with the legacy system.

🎯 Business Context: Why This Mattered

Philips Healthcare faced mounting operational costs from field service visits—each averaging $800-1,500 per trip. With thousands of medical systems worldwide requiring support, even small improvements in remote diagnosis efficiency translated to millions in savings. Additionally, customers demanded faster issue resolution to minimize system downtime in critical healthcare environments.

Problem Definition

Understanding the Multi-Layered Problem

The surface problem was "slow issue resolution," but research revealed a complex ecosystem of technical, organizational, and behavioral challenges.

🏥

Business Pain Points

High operational costs from excessive field visits. Average resolution time of 45-60 minutes per case was 40% above target. Customers dissatisfied with slow turnaround impacting patient care.

👨‍💻

User Frustrations

Engineers forced to navigate 8-12 screens per diagnostic workflow. Critical information buried behind multiple tabs. No integration with complementary analysis tools (CAT, ClickScheduler) forcing constant context switching.

🔧

Technical Constraints

10-year-old legacy codebase with limited documentation. Data scattered across 3 different modality-specific systems (MR, CV, CT). Backend limitations prevented real-time data sync.

🧠

Behavioral Barriers

Deeply entrenched mental models from decade of legacy tool use. Senior engineers (45+ age group) resistant to interface changes. Fear of productivity loss during transition period.

Discovery Phase

Research Methodology: Deep User Immersion

I led a comprehensive 8-week research initiative spanning 3 continents, combining qualitative interviews, contextual inquiry, and quantitative analytics to build a complete picture of the diagnostic workflow.

1

Stakeholder Interviews (2 weeks)

Conducted 12 structured interviews with Portfolio Managers and Business Analysts to understand strategic objectives, cost drivers, and organizational constraints.

Method: Semi-structured interviews (45-60 min each)
  • Portfolio Managers (n=5) across MR, CT, and Cardiovascular modalities
  • Business Analysts (n=4) with cost/efficiency data access
  • Engineering Leads (n=3) understanding technical limitations
Key Questions Asked:
  • What are the current cost drivers in remote support operations?
  • Why has RADAR remained unchanged for 10 years?
  • What would "success" look like for this redesign from a business perspective?
  • Are we improving existing workflows or building new capabilities?
Critical Insights:
  • Frequent field visits ($800-1,500 each) were primary cost concern
  • Business wanted platform upgrade alongside workflow optimization
  • Appetite existed for predictive analytics integration (ISDA tool)
  • Phased rollout essential to minimize disruption risk
2

User Interviews & Contextual Inquiry (4 weeks)

Conducted 32 in-depth interviews and shadowing sessions with end users across their natural work environments in 3 regions: Americas, Europe, and Asia Pacific.

Participants by Role:
  • Remote Service Engineers (n=18) — Primary RADAR users, 10-25 cases/day
  • Field Service Engineers (n=7) — On-site technicians coordinating with remote teams
  • Technical Experts (n=4) — Escalation specialists for complex issues
  • Modality Managers (n=3) — Team leads monitoring regional performance
Research Approach:
  • Semi-structured interviews: Encouraged open conversation while covering key topics
  • Fly-on-the-wall observation: 2-4 hour shadowing sessions watching engineers diagnose real issues
  • Think-aloud protocol: Asked engineers to verbalize their thought process during diagnosis
  • Diary studies: 5 engineers logged workflow pain points for 2 weeks
Interview Tone & Philosophy:

Maintained an attitude of inquiry, treating users as experts in their work. Created psychological safety by acknowledging the legacy tool's longevity ("there must be things it does well—tell me about those"). Looked for non-verbal cues: sighs, eye rolls, workarounds, confessions.

Strategic Design

From Research to Design Principles

Research insights translated into 3 core design principles that guided every subsequent decision, from information architecture to interaction patterns.

💡 How We Reframed the Problem

Initial assumption: "The interface is outdated and hard to use."

After research: "Users have developed efficient workarounds for a rigid system that doesn't adapt to their role, region, or diagnostic workflow. The problem isn't just UI—it's inflexibility."

Strategic bet: Design an adaptive system with role-based defaults and user-level customization, prioritizing information hierarchy optimization over visual refresh.

Design Principles

📊

Structured Information

Critical information upfront with clear priority hierarchy. Users should never wonder "where do I find X?"

Design Implications:
  • System health status visible on dashboard (no drilling required)
  • Visual hierarchy: Errors → Warnings → Info
  • Persistent context panel showing "where am I?"
  • Progressive disclosure for secondary details
🎛️

Adaptable Interface

Interface customizes to user role, region, and preferences. Support diverse workflows without forcing one path.

Design Implications:
  • Role-based login: Engineers see case list, Managers see KPIs
  • Collapsible/expandable sections user can configure
  • Customizable table columns and dashboard widgets
  • Saved preferences persist across sessions
🧭

Optimized Navigation

Navigation matches natural diagnostic workflow sequence. Visual aids guide novices; shortcuts empower experts.

Design Implications:
  • Wizard-like flow for common cases (Auto Mode)
  • Flexible module-based navigation for experts (Manual Mode)
  • Keyboard shortcuts for frequent actions
  • Breadcrumb and system context always visible

Strategic Tradeoffs: What We Consciously Chose

We Prioritized

  • Workflow efficiency over visual novelty
  • Familiar patterns (where users happy) over unnecessary reinvention
  • Customizability over one-size-fits-all simplicity
  • Integration (LOG viewer) over forcing external tool dependence

We Deprioritized

  • Radical visual redesign (change fatigue risk)
  • Mobile responsiveness (desktop-only usage validated)
  • Real-time collaboration (not in user workflows)
  • Predictive mode (MVP scope, tech limitations)

Design Execution

Information Architecture & Interaction Design

Translating principles into structure: navigation models, task flows, and screen-level interaction patterns tested and refined through 4 major iterations.

Phase 1: Information Architecture (3 weeks)

Created modality-specific IA maps for MR, CV (Cardiovascular), and CT systems, identifying shared components and unique requirements.

[IMAGE: Information architecture diagrams for MR and CV modalities showing HOME, SITE OVERVIEW, SYSTEM INFO, SEARCH, DOWNLOADS, etc. hierarchy]

Key IA Decisions

Unified Navigation Model

Consolidated 3 modality-specific navigation structures into single flexible framework. Modality-specific modules (e.g., "Magnet Monitoring" for MR) appear contextually.

Two-Level Hierarchy

Global navigation (Dashboard, Search, Notifications) + System-level tabs (Status, Performance, Config). Eliminated 3rd-level navigation that caused "lost in tabs" problem.

Persistent Context Panel

Left sidebar showing selected system details, health status, quick actions always visible regardless of which tab/page user is on.

Phase 2: Task Flow & State-Transition Diagrams (2 weeks)

Mapped 5 primary diagnostic workflows as detailed flowcharts showing decision points, system states, and alternative paths.

[IMAGE: Task flow diagrams showing: 1. "Auto Mode" workflow: Login → Dashboard → System Selection → Details → Create Case → Close/Next 2. "Manual Mode" workflow: Login → Search by SRN → Extended Details → LOG Viewer → Create Case]
🔄 Workflow Innovation: Three System Modes

Research revealed engineers followed 2 distinct diagnostic patterns:

  • Reactive workflow: Browsing assigned region for systems with issues (Dashboard → Details)
  • Targeted workflow: Direct search by Serial Number for specific case (Search → Details)

We proposed 3 modes (Auto/Manual/Predictive) to optimize each workflow. Due to technical timeline constraints, we delivered Auto + Manual in MVP, with Predictive deferred to Phase 2.

Phase 3: Wireframes & Design Iterations (6 weeks)

Created low-fidelity wireframes for all core screens, tested with 8 representative users, and iterated based on feedback. Below are the key design pivots that shaped the final solution.

Iteration 1 → 2: Dashboard Redesign

  • Critical systems list below fold
  • No at-a-glance KPI summary for managers

✅ V2 Changes Based on Testing
  • Moved location tree to collapsible left sidebar

Iteration 2 → 3: LOG Viewer Integration

The most significant innovation: embedding LOG file analysis directly in RADAR, eliminating need to switch to external CAT tool.

"Wait, I can view and analyze the LOG file without leaving RADAR? This changes everything."

Remote Service Engineer (Usability Test Participant)
Testing Session — Bangalore, India
Design Challenge

LOG files contain 10,000-50,000 lines of system activity. Engineers manually scroll through text files looking for errors—extremely time-consuming.

Inspiration Source

Borrowed visual metaphor from debugging tools like OllyDBG that engineers already use: timeline visualization with interactive cursor and collapsible sections.

Solution Implemented

Visual timeline charting activities/errors, interactive cursor to jump to error line, collapsible "ties" grouping related activities (System Boot → Shutdown), search-in-LOG capability.

Testing Method: Think-Aloud + Task Scenarios

Sample Tasks Given to Participants:
  • Task 1: "You're responsible for Spain. Find all systems in Madrid with critical alerts."
  • Task 2: "System SRN-100200 reported an error. Diagnose the issue using the LOG file."
  • Task 3: "Create a case documenting your findings and mark it for field engineer follow-up."
  • Task 4: "Check how System SRN-68942 performed over the past month."
  • Task 5: "You've resolved the issue. Close the case and move to the next system in your worklist."
Metrics Captured:
  • Task completion rate (success vs. failure)
  • Time on task (compared to legacy baseline)
  • Navigation efficiency (clicks/screens to complete)
  • Error rate (wrong actions, dead ends)
  • Subjective satisfaction (post-task rating 1-10)

Critical Refinements from Testing

  • Added keyboard shortcuts: Users requested Ctrl+N for "next system," Ctrl+S for "create case"
  • Improved error messages: Changed generic "Error loading data" to specific "Cannot reach System SRN-12345. Last ping: 2 hours ago. Retry?"
  • Made sidebar collapsible: Expert users wanted more screen space for main content once region selected
  • Added "Recent Systems": Users frequently returned to same 3-5 systems; added quick access list

Incremental Rollout Strategy & Adoption

To minimize disruption and gather continuous feedback, we rolled out RADAR 2.0 in 6 phases over 18 months while keeping legacy tool operational in parallel.

[IMAGE: Timeline showing rollout phases: Phase 1: Single user MVP (LOG viewer module for MR) Phase 2: MR modality full feature set Phase 3: CV modality addition Phase 4: Create case functionality (MR + CV) Phase 5: User login-based customization Phase 6: Predictive engine integration, full legacy deprecation]
📈 Why Phased Rollout Was Critical to Success

Given user resistance to change, we couldn't force a "big bang" cutover. The phased approach allowed:

  • Continuous validation: Gather feedback after each release, iterate before next phase
  • Confidence building: Early adopters became champions who evangelized to skeptics
  • Risk mitigation: If major issues emerged, users could fall back to legacy tool
  • Training scalability: Onboard users in waves rather than 15,000 simultaneously

Reflection

What I Learned & What I'd Do Differently

Key Lessons

1. Change Management is Design

The biggest barrier wasn't technical—it was behavioral. Investing in user communication, phased rollout, and champion cultivation was as important as the UI itself.

2. Quantify Everything

Data-driven storytelling secured stakeholder buy-in. "Engineers are frustrated" ≠ persuasive. "47% of workflows require tool-switching, costing 8 min per case" = immediate action.

3. Embed with Engineering

Attending daily standups and pairing with developers accelerated implementation and built trust. Design-dev collaboration was key to maintaining quality during build.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Earlier A/B testing: We tested final designs but could have run A/B tests on specific features (e.g., sidebar collapsed vs. expanded default) during beta to optimize further
  • More aggressive predictive mode push: We deferred predictive analytics to Phase 2, but user feedback showed high demand. Should have advocated harder to include in MVP.
  • Automated usability metrics: Wish we'd implemented instrumentation to track task completion time automatically rather than relying on manual observation
  • Internationalization earlier: Didn't account for non-English regions until late. Should have designed with i18n from day 1.

What Came Next

RADAR 2.0's success led to broader platform initiatives:

  • Design system adoption: Component library and patterns reused across Philips' Customer Service Portal and OneEMS tools
  • Predictive diagnostics: Phase 2 integrated ISDA machine learning engine, enabling proactive alerts before failures occur
  • ClickScheduler integration: Bridged RADAR with field service scheduling tool, enabling one-click FSE dispatch
  • My role evolution: Promoted to Senior UX Designer, leading design for 3 enterprise healthcare platforms
