Executive Summary

The Challenge: Breaking Through Legacy Tool Resistance

RADAR was a critical remote diagnostics platform used by 15,000+ service engineers and technical experts globally to troubleshoot medical imaging systems (MRI, CT, X-Ray). The tool had remained unchanged for 10 years, resulting in inefficient workflows, high operational costs, and significant engineer frustration.

As Lead UX Designer managing a team of 4 designers, I led the complete redesign from research through implementation. The challenge wasn't just improving the interface—it was fundamentally rethinking how engineers diagnosed complex medical equipment failures while overcoming deep-rooted resistance to change.

🎯 Business Context: Why This Mattered

Philips Healthcare faced mounting operational costs from field service visits—each averaging $800–1,500 per trip. With thousands of medical systems worldwide requiring support, even small improvements in remote diagnosis efficiency translated to millions in savings. Customers demanded faster issue resolution to minimize downtime in critical healthcare environments.

Problem Definition

Understanding the Multi-Layered Problem

The surface problem was "slow issue resolution," but research revealed a complex ecosystem of technical, organizational, and behavioral challenges.

🏥

Business Pain Points

High operational costs from excessive field visits. Average resolution time of 45–60 minutes per case was 40% above target. Customers dissatisfied with slow turnaround impacting patient care.

👨‍💻

User Frustrations

Engineers forced to navigate 8–12 screens per diagnostic workflow. Critical information buried behind multiple tabs. No integration with complementary tools (CAT, ClickScheduler), forcing constant context switching.

🔧

Technical Constraints

10-year-old legacy codebase with limited documentation. Data scattered across 3 different modality-specific systems (MR, CV, CT). Backend limitations prevented real-time data sync.

🧠

Behavioral Barriers

Deeply entrenched mental models from a decade of legacy tool use. Senior engineers (45+ age group) resistant to interface changes. Fear of productivity loss during transition period.

Discovery Phase

Research Methodology: Deep User Immersion

I led a comprehensive 8-week research initiative spanning 3 continents, combining qualitative interviews, contextual inquiry, and quantitative analytics.

1

Stakeholder Interviews (2 weeks)

12 structured interviews with Portfolio Managers and Business Analysts to understand strategic objectives, cost drivers, and organizational constraints.

Participants:
  • Portfolio Managers (n=5) across MR, CT, and Cardiovascular modalities
  • Business Analysts (n=4) with cost/efficiency data access

Critical Insights:

32 in-depth interviews and shadowing sessions across 3 regions: Americas, Europe, and Asia Pacific.

Participants by Role:

Research Approach:
  • Semi-structured interviews: Encouraged open conversation while covering key topics

Research Synthesis

Affinity Mapping: 3 Core Theme Clusters

A 2-day synthesis workshop with the design team, PM, and stakeholders clustered 240+ interview insights into actionable themes.

🏗️ Structured Information

  • Critical info hidden behind 4–5 clicks
  • Information hierarchy mismatched with user priorities
  • Irrelevant data shown upfront (all countries when user only needs Spain)
  • No way to customize dashboard view
  • Lost context when switching between tabs

🎨 Adaptable Interface

  • No personalization based on user role/region
  • Cannot customize table columns or layout
  • All settings reset on re-login
  • Interface forces one workflow path for all engineers
  • No "favorites" or recent systems quick access

🧭 Optimized Navigation

  • Navigation doesn't match diagnostic workflow sequence
  • Novice users get lost; expert users lack shortcuts
  • Location tree navigation rarely used (everyone uses search)
  • No visual breadcrumb: "Where am I? Which site is selected?"
  • Keyboard shortcuts non-existent despite expert demand

Strategic Design

From Research to Design Principles

Research insights translated into 3 core design principles that guided every subsequent decision.

💡 How We Reframed the Problem

Initial assumption: "The interface is outdated and hard to use."

After research: "Users have developed efficient workarounds for a rigid system that doesn't adapt to their role, region, or diagnostic workflow. The problem isn't just UI — it's inflexibility."

Strategic bet: Design an adaptive system with role-based defaults and user-level customization, prioritizing information hierarchy optimization over visual refresh.

📊

Structured Information

Critical information upfront with clear priority hierarchy. Users should never wonder "where do I find X?"

  • System health visible on dashboard (no drilling)
  • Visual hierarchy: Errors → Warnings → Info
  • Persistent context panel always visible
  • Progressive disclosure for secondary details
🎛️

Adaptable Interface

Interface customizes to user role, region, and preferences. Support diverse workflows without forcing one path.

  • Role-based login: Engineers see case list, Managers see KPIs
  • Collapsible/expandable sections
  • Customizable table columns and widgets
  • Saved preferences persist across sessions
🧭

Optimized Navigation

Navigation matches natural diagnostic workflow sequence. Visual aids guide novices; shortcuts empower experts.

  • Wizard-like flow for common cases (Auto Mode)
  • Flexible module-based navigation (Manual Mode)
  • Keyboard shortcuts for frequent actions
  • Breadcrumb and system context always visible

✅ We Prioritized

  • Workflow efficiency over visual novelty
  • Familiar patterns over unnecessary reinvention
  • Customizability over one-size-fits-all simplicity
  • LOG viewer integration over external tool dependence

❌ We Deprioritized

  • Radical visual redesign (change fatigue risk)
  • Mobile responsiveness (desktop-only validated)
  • Real-time collaboration (not in workflows)
  • Predictive mode (deferred to Phase 2)
Design Execution

Information Architecture & Interaction Design

Translating principles into structure: navigation models, task flows, and screen-level interaction patterns — tested and refined through 4 major iterations.

Phase 1: Information Architecture (3 weeks)

Created modality-specific IA maps for MR, CV (Cardiovascular), and CT systems, identifying shared components and unique requirements.

Unified Navigation Model

Consolidated 3 modality-specific navigation structures into a single flexible framework. Modality-specific modules (e.g., "Magnet Monitoring" for MR) appear contextually.

Two-Level Hierarchy

Global navigation (Dashboard, Search, Notifications) + System-level tabs (Status, Performance, Config). Eliminated 3rd-level navigation that caused "lost in tabs" problem.

Persistent Context Panel

Left sidebar showing selected system details, health status, quick actions — always visible regardless of which tab/page user is on.

[IMAGE: Information architecture diagrams for MR and CV modalities — HOME, SITE OVERVIEW, SYSTEM INFO, SEARCH, DOWNLOADS hierarchy]

Phase 2: Task Flow & State-Transition Diagrams (2 weeks)

Mapped 5 primary diagnostic workflows as detailed flowcharts showing decision points, system states, and alternative paths.

🔄 Workflow Innovation: Three System Modes

Research revealed 2 distinct diagnostic patterns engineers used:

  • Reactive workflow: Browsing assigned region for systems with issues (Dashboard → Details)
  • Targeted workflow: Direct search by Serial Number for a specific case (Search → Details)

We proposed 3 modes (Auto/Manual/Predictive). Due to technical constraints, Auto + Manual were delivered in MVP; Predictive deferred to Phase 2.

[IMAGE: Task flow diagrams — "Auto Mode": Login → Dashboard → System Selection → Details → Create Case → Close/Next | "Manual Mode": Login → Search by SRN → Extended Details → LOG Viewer → Create Case]

Iteration 2 → 3: LOG Viewer Integration

The most significant innovation: embedding LOG file analysis directly in RADAR, eliminating the need to switch to the external CAT tool.

Design Challenge

LOG files contain 10,000–50,000 lines of system activity. Engineers manually scrolled through text files looking for errors — extremely time-consuming.

Inspiration Source

Borrowed visual metaphor from debugging tools like OllyDBG: timeline visualization with interactive cursor and collapsible sections.

Solution Implemented

Visual timeline charting activities/errors, interactive cursor to jump to error line, collapsible "ties" grouping related activities, search-in-LOG capability.

Iteration 3 → 4: System Details Calendar View

Reflection

What I Learned & What I'd Do Differently

1. Change Management is Design

The biggest barrier wasn't technical — it was behavioral. Investing in user communication, phased rollout, and champion cultivation was as important as the UI itself.

2. Quantify Everything

Data-driven storytelling secured buy-in. "Engineers are frustrated" ≠ persuasive. "47% of workflows require tool-switching, costing 8 min per case" = immediate action.

3. Embed with Engineering

Attending daily standups and pairing with developers accelerated implementation and built trust. Design-dev collaboration was key to maintaining quality.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Earlier A/B testing: Could have run A/B tests on specific features (sidebar collapsed vs. expanded default) during beta
  • More aggressive predictive mode push: User feedback showed high demand — should have advocated harder for MVP inclusion
  • Automated usability metrics: Instrumentation to track task completion time automatically vs. manual observation
  • Internationalization earlier: Should have designed with i18n from day 1 for non-English regions

What Came Next

  • Design system adoption: Reused across Philips Customer Service Portal and OneEMS
  • Predictive diagnostics: Phase 2 integrated ISDA ML engine, enabling proactive alerts before failures
  • ClickScheduler integration: One-click FSE dispatch from within RADAR
  • My role evolution: Promoted to Senior UX Designer, leading design for 3 enterprise healthcare platforms
