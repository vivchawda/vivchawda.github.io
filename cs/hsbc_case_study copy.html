Project Overview

This case study covers the redesign of a mobile wealth-management experience for HSBC, focusing on two core areas: an Insights & Knowledge Center that educates retail investors through curated content, and a Customer Dashboard that provides a holistic view of financial health with actionable next steps.

The goal was to transform static educational content and dense dashboards into a guided, personalized experience that drives financial literacy and product adoption while remaining accessible for both novice and experienced investors.

The Challenge

Retail investors in India increasingly manage their finances through mobile apps, yet current solutions fall short:

  • Siloed learning: Educational content is disconnected from transactional flows, making it hard to act on insights
  • Number overload: Dashboards present data without clear guidance on what to do next
  • Trust erosion: Gamified features often feel promotional rather than genuinely educational

Research & Discovery

I conducted moderated 1:1 usability sessions with 20 participants, combining task-based testing with mental model exploration. The research focused on comprehension, navigation, trust, and relevance of insights and dashboard widgets.

Research Objectives

  • Understand how users discover and evaluate educational content in financial apps
  • Test navigation and filtering mechanisms across different content types
  • Assess trust signals and perception of promotional vs. educational content
  • Validate dashboard information hierarchy and actionability
Participant Composition

Investment confidence: 15% low confidence, 45% medium, 30% high, 10% very high

Experience levels: 30% beginners (0‚Äì1 year), 40% intermediate (1‚Äì3 years), 20% advanced (3‚Äì7 years), 10% very advanced (7+ years)

Content preference: 40% prefer videos, 35% prefer articles, 15% news summaries, 10% advisor calls

Dashboard-Specific Findings

Financial Overview Clarity

85% could quickly read net worth in the updated white version, feeling it was "cleaner" and "less intimidating" than the dark original. However, 25% felt it looked like "a marketing web page, not my personal finance."

Next Action Discovery

70% successfully used "Check your financial health score" as their next step, but 30% were unsure whether to scroll or tap a specific card for guidance.

Advisor Access Hidden

Only 45% noticed the "Advisor connect" floating button, with 55% thinking it was a dismissible notification rather than a persistent support entry point.

Payment Shortcuts Buried

While all participants understood the "Easy payments" row, 40% wished it appeared higher on the screen, closer to frequent tasks.

Design Principles

Research insights translated into five core UX principles that guided all design decisions:

1

Make expertise levels explicit

Clearly segment content into "Start here" vs "Go deeper" with prominent, high-contrast labels that users can scan immediately.

2

Surface outcome-oriented summaries

Lead with "What does this mean for me?" and "What should I do?" rather than presenting plain news or data.

3

Frame games as learning tools

Position gamification with clear learning promises and risk-free framing to build trust, not skepticism.

4

Dashboard as decision surface

Every key metric should connect to 1‚Äì2 recommended actions (improve score, rebalance, protect risk).

5

Respect cognitive load

Financial data is inherently complex; visual hierarchy, whitespace, and progressive disclosure are critical.

  • Entry points for educational content from transactional areas

High-Fidelity Design: Visual Language

With structure validated, I applied HSBC's brand language while introducing modern patterns for wealth management:

  • Primary: HSBC red (#DB0011) for brand continuity and key CTAs
  • Surfaces: Deep charcoal gradients for premium dark mode; off-white for accessibility in light mode
  • Semantic colors: Green for gains/positive trends, red for losses/alerts, teal/coral for secondary actions

Key Design Decisions

Several design decisions directly addressed research findings and balanced user needs with business objectives:

1. Narrative Home Hero Instead of Pure Numbers

Problem: Users found dense financial data intimidating and struggled to understand "am I doing well?"

Solution: Introduced contextual greetings ("Good morning, Astha!"), trend arrows with color-coded rings, and simplified percentage indicators to make the overview more human and scannable.

Impact: 90% of users in follow-up testing could assess their financial health in under 10 seconds.

2. Goal-Centric "Explore Promises"

Problem: Users thought in terms of life goals ("saving for a house") rather than product types ("mutual funds").

Solution: Grouped products under aspirational goals (Home, Retirement, Travel, Education) with icon-based visual language and storytelling copy.

Impact: Reduced cognitive load and created emotional connection to financial planning.

3. Inline Financial Fitness Nudges

Problem: Users avoided complex financial assessments, viewing them as time-consuming obligations.

Solution: Positioned credit score and health assessment as lightweight, scannable cards early in dashboard scroll with clear benefit statements ("Unlock better credit card offers").

Impact: Reframed assessments as opportunities rather than homework.

4. Curated Insights Carousel

Problem: Educational content felt disconnected from personal finances.

Solution: Created contextual content cards on the dashboard ("7 facts about EV bikes" after viewing auto loan products) with horizontal scrolling for easy browsing.

Impact: Bridged the gap between learning and action with minimal friction.

5. Persistent But Unobtrusive Advisor Access

Problem: Users missed advisor access or thought it was a dismissible notification.

Solution: Redesigned as a clearly labeled floating chip ("Advisor connect") that remains visible during scroll but doesn't block core tasks.

Impact: Maintained high-touch support for users who need it without cluttering the experience.

6. Explicit Content Skill Levels

Problem: 65% of users missed skill level labels, leading to avoidance of appropriate content.

Solution: Moved level badges (Beginner, Intermediate, Advanced) to the top of content cards with increased size, contrast, and icon support. Added "Start here" section for first-time investors.

Impact: Content discovery improved significantly, with users confidently selecting appropriate resources.

Addressing UX Risks

Throughout the design process, I identified potential risks and built in mitigation strategies:

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk: Sales Push Perception

Challenge: Users perceive prominent product recommendations as advertising

Mitigation: Added transparency labels ("Educational game ‚Äì uses virtual money only"), toned down urgency language ("MUST TRY" ‚Üí "Recommended for you"), and included clear benefit statements with risk disclosures

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk: Overwhelming First-Time Users

Challenge: Dense dashboards can intimidate beginners

Mitigation: Introduced optional onboarding tour highlighting 3‚Äì4 essential areas, progressive disclosure with "See more" expanders, and contextual tooltips for advanced features

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk: Content Silos

Challenge: Educational content remains disconnected from transactional flows

Mitigation: Added "Related insights" under product cards, context-aware deep links from dashboard to hub, and "Learn more about this" buttons on complex products

What I Would Do Next

If the project continued, I would focus on three key areas to further improve engagement, personalization, and conversion:

A. Enhanced Insights & Knowledge Center

  • Stronger segmentation: Add a "Start here" strip with 3‚Äì5 beginner tiles ("Investing basics", "Mutual funds 101", "Tax-saving strategies") for first-time users
  • "For you" personalization: Create a dedicated tab prioritizing insights linked to user holdings and risk profile with contextual tags like "Because you invest in equity funds‚Ä¶"
  • Advanced filtering: Allow users to filter by skill level (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced) across all media types simultaneously
  • Gamification transparency: Reframe "Play & Learn" as "Practice investing with virtual money" with a micro-panel explaining rules and learning objectives
  • Progression tracking: Add badges and levels tied to actual learning outcomes ("Completed Basics of Equity", "Tax Planning Master")

B. Smarter Customer Dashboard

  • Personalized "What matters now": Show 3 context-aware chips immediately below hero ("3 SIPs due this week", "Credit score changed +20", "Rebalance recommended in equity funds")
  • Flexible dashboard structure: Let users collapse sections they care less about and persist these preferences; consider sticky sub-navigation for Overview, Goals, Insights, Products, Payments
  • Clearer advisor access: Replace floating bubble with a labeled bottom sheet entry ("Talk to an advisor") opening structured options: schedule call, chat, FAQs
  • Payment shortcuts elevation: Move "Easy payments" closer to top or provide quick toggle between "Investments" and "Payments" views since many sessions start transactional
  • Comprehensive empty states: Design explicit states for no investments, pending KYC, or very low balances with guided checklists ("3 steps to get started")

C. Measurement & Iteration

  • Content engagement metrics: Track completion rates, time spent, and save/share actions by skill level and content type
  • Conversion tracking: Measure how many users move from educational content to product exploration and actual transactions
  • A/B testing: Test variations of CTA language, content card layouts, and dashboard module ordering to optimize for both learning and conversion
  • Qualitative follow-ups: Conduct quarterly diary studies to understand how investment confidence evolves with app usage
Target Outcomes

With these improvements, I would aim for:

  • 20% increase in content engagement (time spent in Insights hub)
  • 5% lift in product cross-sell from dashboard recommendations
  • 30% improvement in first-time investor confidence scores
  • 15% reduction in advisor dependency for basic investment decisions

Key Learnings

This project reinforced several critical principles for designing financial products:

1

Trust is the foundation

In financial products, even subtle promotional language can erode trust. Transparency, clear sourcing, and regulatory compliance should be visible, not buried.

2

Education must be contextual

Users don't visit separate "learning centers"‚Äîthey learn in the moment of need. Deep links between dashboards and content are essential.

3

Progressive disclosure manages complexity

Financial data is inherently complex. Starting simple and revealing depth on demand prevents overwhelming users while serving power users.

4

Mental models over product taxonomy

Users think in goals ("buy a house") not products ("mutual funds"). Organizing around aspirations creates emotional connection and clarity.
